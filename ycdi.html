Meet Our Puppet Friends!

Each puppet teaches tamariki a vital social-emotional skill

Organisation

Oscar Organisation

Oscar helps tamariki learn to plan, set goals, and manage their time and belongings. He shows children that being organised helps them feel calm, ready, and in control.

Planning Goal-setting
Confidence

Connie Confidence

Connie encourages tamariki to believe in themselves, try new things, and speak up. She teaches children that making mistakes is part of learning and that they are capable and worthy.

Self-belief Courage
Getting Along

Gabby Get Along

Gabby shows tamariki how to make friends, share, take turns, and resolve conflict kindly. She models how to listen, empathise, and be a caring member of the community.

Friendship Empathy
Persistence

Pete Persistence

Pete inspires tamariki to keep trying even when things feel hard. He teaches that effort and practice help us grow, and that giving up too soon means missing out on the joy of achieving.

Effort Growth mindset
Resilience

Ricky Resilience

Ricky helps tamariki bounce back from disappointment and setbacks. He shows children how to manage big feelings, ask for help, and find their strength again after something goes wrong.

Bounce-back Emotions

Design Your YCDI! Curriculum

Three ways to weave YCDI into your everyday learning environment

👀

Make it Visual

Role-model positive attitudes using the YCDI puppets so tamariki can see what each skill looks like in action. Display puppet posters, Y-Charts, and classroom visual cues at child level. Use photos of tamariki demonstrating positive attitudes, and create documentation panels that celebrate YCDI moments in your learning environment.

  • ✓ Puppet displays at child height
  • ✓ Y-Charts for each skill
  • ✓ Photo documentation panels
  • ✓ Puppet in key learning areas
🔊

Make it Heard

Use behaviour-specific feedback and the language of YCDI throughout the day. Introduce YCDI songs, stories, and read-alouds that reinforce the 5 skills. Share the puppet voices — let Oscar, Connie, Gabby, Pete, and Ricky narrate challenges and victories. Celebrate effort and attitude out loud with tamariki and whānau.

  • ✓ Behaviour-specific feedback
  • ✓ YCDI songs and chants
  • ✓ Read-alouds from book list
  • ✓ Puppet storytelling sessions
🤲

Make it Felt

Teach tamariki to recognise and name their feelings, and practise self-talk strategies. Use art media, dramatic play, and collaborative projects to embed positive attitudes experientially. Connect with whānau so the YCDI language and attitudes extend into home life, creating a consistent and nurturing environment across both settings.

  • ✓ Emotion recognition activities
  • ✓ Self-talk strategy practice
  • ✓ Art & dramatic play
  • ✓ Whānau connection strategies

Success Stories

Real moments of YCDI in action across our centres

Ricky Resilience
Wellington Centre · Ruma Pūāwai
Resilience

"Ricky says we don't give up!"

🧩

Little Aroha, age 4, used to become very distressed when puzzles were difficult — often throwing pieces or walking away in tears. After the kaiako team introduced Ricky Resilience over three weeks, including daily role-play with the puppet, something shifted. One morning, as Aroha struggled with a 24-piece puzzle, her key teacher watched as she quietly told herself: "Ricky says breathe and try again." She completed it, then turned and helped a friend who was also struggling, saying "It's okay, we don't give up."

Felt Resilience Peer support
Gabby Get Along
Auckland Centre · Ruma Kāhui
Getting Along

From conflict to collaboration

🤝

Two boys in the 4-year-old group were constantly clashing over the block area. Kaiako introduced Gabby Get Along through puppet sessions and a Gabby corner in the block area. Within a fortnight, the boys had created their own "Gabby rules" for sharing the blocks, writing and illustrating them with the kaiako's help. They proudly showed whānau at pick-up time: "We made the rules together like Gabby taught us."

Heard Visual Getting Along
Connie Confidence
Christchurch Centre · Ruma Tūī
Confidence

A quiet voice finds its roar

🎤

Mei, age 3½, rarely spoke above a whisper and avoided group activities. Her kaiako used Connie Confidence in one-on-one puppet play for two weeks before introducing group sessions. By week six, Mei was volunteering to hold Connie during mat time and speak for the puppet — something that would have been unimaginable before. Her mum cried at the end-of-year celebration when Mei performed a YCDI song with the group. "Connie makes me brave," Mei told her.

Felt Confidence Whānau connection
Pete Persistence
Hamilton Centre · Ruma Kererū
Persistence

The longest road to tying shoelaces

👟

Four-year-old Tama wanted to tie his own shoelaces but found the fine motor task incredibly frustrating. His kaiako introduced Pete Persistence, sharing the puppet's mantra: "I can do hard things — I just haven't done them yet." They documented Tama's 11-day journey through photos. On day 11, Tama tied both laces, stood up, and punched the air yelling "PETE! I DID IT!" The whole room erupted. The photo series became a documentation panel that inspired three other children to attempt their own "hard thing" challenges.
